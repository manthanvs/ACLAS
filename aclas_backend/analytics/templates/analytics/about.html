About ACLAS

How ACLAS Works

ACLAS helps you understand your coding habits. It quietly watches how you work in your editor, figures out when things get frustrating, and shows you simple summaries so you can improve your workflow without the guesswork.

🔗

How It Works

From typing to tracking — in 5 simple steps

1

Your Editor Keeps an Eye Out

Our free extension runs quietly in your code editor. Every few seconds, it notices things like code errors, how often you switch files or undo typing, and if your code fails to run. It also tracks how long you're actively typing versus taking a break.

2

Sending the Quick Notes

The extension packages up these quick notes and securely sends them to the ACLAS server using your personal connection key. We call each set of notes an "Event".

3

Safe Storage

The server receives your data, double-checks it, and saves it securely in a database. Everything is linked only to your account, keeping your info totally private.

4

Connecting the Dots & Checking Stress

When you open your Dashboard, the system looks at all your past events. It crunches the numbers and calculates a "Stress Score" to figure out which coding moments were the most frustrating.

5

Your Personal Dashboard

Finally, the data is turned into easy-to-read charts. You can see your favorite programming languages, your most active projects, and pinpoint exactly when and why coding got stressful.

What We Track

The basic details of every coding session

🕐 Active Time
Time spent actively typing and writing code.
💤 Idle Time
Time spent paused, reading, or stepping away from the keyboard.
➕ Lines Added
New lines of code you successfully wrote.
➖ Lines Deleted
Lines you removed (lots of deleting might mean you're re-thinking things!).
🗣️ Language
The programming language you were using (like Python or JavaScript).
📁 Project
The name of the folder or project you were working on.

What Causes "Stress"?

The signs we use to figure out if you're feeling stuck

🔴 Errors Important
Code mistakes popping up while you type.
🔁 Repeated Errors Very Important
The same error staying there for a while — a big sign you might be stuck.
💥 Build Failures Very Important
When you try to run or test your program and it completely crashes.
🔄 File Switches Minor
Jumping between different files quickly, which might mean you're a bit lost.
↩️ Undo Count Noticeable
Hitting Ctrl+Z a lot, meaning you're changing your mind or fixing mistakes.
🖥️ Terminal Errors Important
Commands failing when you type them into the terminal window.

ℹ️ Successful Builds are tracked, but they don't cause stress! Testing your code often is a good habit. We only add stress points if the build fails.

🧮

How We Calculate Stress

Keeping the math simple and fair

Basically, we take all the "Stress" signs mentioned above (like errors and undos) and add them up. But some signs are more frustrating than others — for example, a program crashing is much more annoying than hitting Ctrl+Z once. So, we give the tough problems a higher "weight" to reflect reality.

-- The plain English version of our math --
1. Point System: Every error, crash, and undo gives you stress points.

2. Fairness Check: We divide your total points by the amount of time you spent coding, so longer sessions don't automatically look worse than shorter ones.

3. Maximum Score: We cap the final score at 100, giving you a percentage gauge that is super easy to understand.

The end result is an easy 0 to 100 number that instantly tells you: "Oh, things were getting pretty rough there!" or "That was a smooth coding session."

🚦

Stress Levels Explained

What the numbers actually mean

0 – 30 🟢 Low Stress Smooth sailing! A few errors are normal.
31 – 60 🟡 Medium Stress Some bumps in the road. You might be figuring out a tricky problem.
61 – 100 🔴 High Stress Frustrating. Multiple things are failing or you might be stuck.

Don't worry if you hit Medium or High stress occasionally! Coding is hard work. This tool is just here to help you spot when you might need to take a break or ask for help.
